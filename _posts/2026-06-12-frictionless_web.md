every interface you touched today was designed to be forgotten, ignored, and utilized to the point of brutalizing beauty. the goal of modern UI design is friction-free consumption: get the user in, strip away anything that requires a second glance or thoughtful consideration, and direct their attention somewhere it can be efficiently exploited. **we are living in an era of digital brutalism disguised as minimalism**. everything is an inoffensive, rounded rectangle in different shades of white. a muted accent color as a facade for character or personality. strictly utilitarian. 

but a personal website shouldn't be sterile. it shouldn't be optimized for efficient user exploitation. it should be a place for expression, emotion, a cozy digital living room that you would want to invite guests into.

# breaking the grid

nature doesnвҖҷt use grid systems, yet it achieves a perfect, chaotic balance. when we build for the web, we instantly trap our brains inside the CSS box model. we think in terms of columns, flexbox containers, and padding. we are constrained by imaginary lines, literally thinking inside the box. what happens when we intentionally break those boundaries?

we have been conditioned to view screens as flat, predictable surfaces where every pixel must be accounted for and justified by utility. but when we look at the physical spaces that actually bring us comfort, like a cluttered bookshelf, a worn-in leather armchair, or the uneven canopy of a forest, **their beauty lies entirely in their lack of standardization**. a personal website should reflect that same human irregularity. it should be a space where elements are allowed to lean against one another, where margins aren't just empty padding but breathing room, and where the layout feels like it was assembled by hand over time rather than generated by a sterile layout engine. by intentionally stepping outside the rigid constraints of modern web standards, we can start treating the browser less like a spreadsheet and more like a canvas.

the web is a malleable medium, yet we treat it like a rigid sheet of plastic. we have forgotten how to let our digital spaces grow wild.

# the era of the flat world

we tend to incorrectly write it off as nostalgia, but if you take a moment to consider the past eras of our world, it becomes incredibly obvious that weвҖҷve traded wonder for efficiency. everywhere we look, the world has fallen victim to a relentless, aggressive oversimplification. we used to build things with the intention of them being looked at, handled, and admired. fence posts used to be beautiful, detailed stretches of wrought iron that framed a house and gave it character... now theyвҖҷre just straight, ugly metal beams or, worse, chain link. phone booths used to be vibrant, detailed, iconic structures that gave a street corner a sense of place... now theyвҖҷre gone, replaced by sterile, colorless glass and plastic. we can admire the architecture of old cities - the gorgeous stone carvings, the gargoyles, and the intricate masonry. it used to tell a story that has now been completely erased in favor of monoliths of grey concrete and mirrored glass. we are systematically stripping the texture out of the physical world because it's cheaper and faster to build things that feel like **nothing**.

the web has suffered the exact same fate. we have collective amnesia about what the internet used to be, willingly trading a beautiful, enjoyable experience for brutalist utility and frictionless consumption. websites aren't allowed to be landing pages anymore. they arenвҖҷt allowed to be digital front yards or cozy living rooms where youвҖҷre invited to sit down and stay awhile. instead, theyвҖҷve been reduced to functional tools, optimized to get you in, extract your data or your money, and push you out as efficiently as possible. weвҖҷve let the entire digital landscape be flattened into a series of identical, utilitarian templates under the guise of "good UX," forgetting that the best user experiences are the ones that actually make you think and feel.

# textures over blank spaces

why did we decide that textures were the enemy? in the early 2000s, the web was full of terrible skeuomorphism, fake leather backgrounds and glossy glass buttons. we had clashing colors, flashing text, volumetric buttons, and layered windows. but we overcorrected.
now, everything looks like it was manufactured in a sterile cleanroom. we need a middle ground. i want to see digital dust. i want background patterns that mimic the grain of heavy cold-press paper or the damp irregularities of moss on a tree trunk. when you look at a screen, your eyes should feel like they are touching something, not just scanning an emissive display. we need **texture** back on our web.

# in conclusion...

the web isn't a product, nor is it a tool. the web is a canvas - meant for expression, sharing stories and information, taking people to a different world. it is the most versitile medium we have, more interactive than movies, paintings, and stories, larger than any sketchbook or canvas. we have endless tools at our disposal, and reducing it to a capitalistic system is a failure of imagination.
